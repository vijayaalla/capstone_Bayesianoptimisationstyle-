# Module 24 Final Discussion Post

Over the course of the BBO capstone, my project evolved from a simple exploratory baseline into a more structured optimisation pipeline. The codebase was built mainly from scratch rather than copied from a full public BO framework. I used `numpy`, `scikit-learn`, and `scipy` for the core modeling pieces, but the repository structure, query-generation scripts, and reporting workflow were my own. I chose that route because I wanted a setup I could understand end to end, adapt every week, and explain clearly in the discussion posts. The public repository is here: https://github.com/vijayaalla/capstone_Bayesianoptimisationstyle-

My starting point was intentionally simple: a Gaussian Process baseline with a UCB-style acquisition rule in `src/generate_round_queries.py`. That was a sensible first choice because the challenge had sparse data, expensive queries, and a strict sequential budget of one new point per function per round. From there, the code changed almost every week. Early rounds moved from manual heuristics to GP-guided selection, then to a bootstrap SVR ensemble in Week 3 and an MLP ensemble in Week 4. The reason for those changes was that I wanted more flexibility for nonlinear functions while still keeping some estimate of uncertainty. In Week 5, I added a deep-ensemble style search with a novelty bonus and local refinement so that the pipeline did not keep proposing points that were too similar to earlier ones. In Week 6, I made what felt like the first major architectural jump: a HEBO-inspired hybrid pipeline with input warping and a blended acquisition based on EI, PI, UCB, and uncertainty. That change mattered because it turned the project from “one model, one score” into a more robust decision system.

The later weeks were less about adding complexity for its own sake and more about making the system more disciplined. In Week 7, I added per-function hyperparameter tuning and surrogate-family selection using cross-validated MAE. That was one of the most important improvements because it replaced a one-size-fits-all approach with evidence-based model choice. In Weeks 8 and 9, I experimented with LLM-inspired and scaling-inspired decision layers, which pushed me to think about candidate selection as a structured reasoning process rather than just an argmax over one acquisition value. In Week 10, I simplified that thinking into a transparency-first scoring rule built from predicted value, uncertainty, and novelty. In Weeks 11 and 12, I added clustering and PCA-guided reasoning to identify local groups, dominant directions, and redundant dimensions. Finally, in Week 13, I framed the last round through an RL-inspired policy comparison between exploit, explore, and PCA-guided arms. Looking back, the changes with the strongest impact were the move to hybrid/tuned surrogates and the shift toward transparent, per-function decision rules. Those changes improved not only the query quality, but also my confidence in why each query had been selected.

In terms of final result, the closing leaderboard showed a mixed but useful pattern rather than a dramatic late jump. Across the 65 participants listed, my strongest relative results were Function 8, where I ranked 24th, and Function 4, where I ranked 27th. Function 5 was closer to the middle at 35th, while Functions 2 and 7 were weaker at 51st. That suggests some of my later strategies worked better when the search could exploit clearer local structure or dominant directions, but were less consistent on functions where uncertainty stayed high for longer. I should still be transparent here: the public repository does not contain a complete machine-readable log of every returned portal score, so I cannot reconstruct an exact week-by-week score curve from the repo alone. What I can say is that the final phase felt more stable and more exploitative than the middle of the project. By Week 10, the workflow had become more reproducible; by Week 11 and Week 12, the search was focusing on promising clusters and principal directions rather than broad random coverage; and in Week 13, the exploit arm was selected for all eight functions in the final RL-inspired policy report. To me, that suggests the evidence had become strong enough that local refinement was more justified than broad exploration, even if that did not translate into equally strong leaderboard placements on every function. If I had more time or a fresh start, I would do three things differently: log every returned portal score automatically, keep a single performance dashboard across all rounds, and test a more specialised BO stack such as BoTorch or TuRBO for the higher-dimensional functions.

The biggest trade-offs throughout the capstone were exploration versus exploitation, simplicity versus flexibility, and short-term gains versus long-term learning. Early in the project, I leaned more toward exploration because uncertainty was dominating the decision process. Later, my understanding shifted to “exploit when the evidence repeats, but keep a measured check on alternatives.” That is why the final round still compared exploit, explore, and PCA-guided strategies instead of assuming the best current region should always win by default. Another trade-off was model sophistication versus trustworthiness. Some of the more elaborate ideas were interesting, but not all of them improved decisions as much as simpler additions like tuning, novelty constraints, or clearer scoring rules. I also had to balance weekly experimentation with continuity. Restarting from a completely new strategy every week might have been exciting, but it would have made the process less cumulative. The better choice was to treat each round as an update to a stable pipeline.

The most important lesson I learned is that in black-box optimisation, good process matters as much as good predictions. The strongest improvements did not come from chasing the most fashionable method, but from building a workflow that could learn from feedback, manage uncertainty, and stay reproducible under limited data. Each new round acted like a reward update: strong outputs increased my confidence in some regions and strategy styles, while weak outputs reduced their value. That is why the project increasingly reminded me of reinforcement learning, although not in the full sequential-control sense. More precisely, the early rounds were closer to a bandit-style or model-free update process, because I was learning directly from trial-and-error feedback, while the later rounds became more model-based because surrogate models were used to anticipate which queries were worth trying next. So I would describe the capstone as black-box optimisation with RL-inspired decision logic rather than full reinforcement learning. In future competitions or real-world ML projects, I would apply this lesson by starting with a strong baseline, introducing complexity only when it changes decisions, and keeping better logs of both model fit and downstream outcomes.

What surprised me most was that the most useful gains often came from structure rather than headline complexity. I expected the more sophisticated models to dominate, but in practice some of the biggest improvements came from per-function model selection, distance filtering, novelty bonuses, and clearer reasoning about when to stop exploring. I was also surprised by how quickly the problem changed character as more data accumulated: early rounds felt like searching in the dark, while the final rounds felt more like evidence-weighted refinement. That shift made the exploration-exploitation trade-off much more concrete for me, and it is probably the part of the capstone that I will carry forward most strongly.
